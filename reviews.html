Poor Things

Dir. Yorgos Lanthimos • 2/5
Poor Things is a beautifully shot, wonderfully acted, and amazingly designed movie that had me smiling in awe during its first half. The story is fresh, the color palette is delicious, the set design is wondrous, and the acting is fantastic—yet only for the first half, and that is where the movie should’ve ended.

For the second half, it struggles with a grinding, repetitive plot. It is here, too, that the movie moves from a neo-Gothic, somehow more perverse retelling of Frankenstein into yet another exploitative, male-led, male-written explication of what women’s liberation should look like: that women can only find self-realization by offering (or in this case, selling) themselves to men and becoming commodities, or, as the movie puts it, “their own means of production.” In this view, all men are violent and depraved beasts. It is a plot that borders on the pedophilic and forces the audience to consider, disgustingly, if a woman with the mind of a child is being raped or not; it carries the implication that a good partner is one who accepts all your actions without judgment or opposition, regardless of how it affects them.

Ultimately, turn the movie off at the halfway mark. If you do not, you won’t be made to question, to wonder, or to reflect… you’ll just be sick.

Stoner

John Williams • 4.5/5
A remarkable novel about a remarkably unremarkable man. In this, it embodies its story. Both the novel and its author are often ignored when discussing the great works—and workers—of literature.

William Stoner is no hero, he is not the smartest man in the room, nor the most successful; he simply is. In this, there is a great comfort—that our lives don’t need to be a grand adventure for them to be worthy of telling. That you don’t need to be great to be anything at all.

John Williams takes you on this journey with beautiful but economic prose. You will be left conflicted, frustrated, emotional, but never lost. This is a must-read for anyone with even a passing interest in the greatest works of literature.
