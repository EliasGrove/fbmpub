National Socialists

TL;DR

The erasure of the "other" is foundational, not reactionary. Total systems cannot accommodate internal contradictions.

In a 2017 BBC article titled "The Endless Influence of Bauhaus," Mr. William Cook asks: "Why did the Nazis feel so threatened by the Bauhaus? Why were they so scared of an art school that made modernist furniture and kitchenware?"

Mr. Cook answers his own question: "Because it represented a worldview which was the complete opposite of National Socialism." This is a misconception both in essence and in effect.

In essence because National Socialists didn't see Bauhaus as the "complete opposite" of their ideology. Bauhaus products like Marianne Brandt's lamps remained in production, and former Bauhaus students held reputable positions in the regime.

In effect, because it wasn't out of fear. For seventy-odd years, historians have portrayed extremist political movements as nervous, prickly little things that ban, shoot, and imprison "otherness" out of fear—a fundamental misunderstanding of both the resilience of these movements and the conditions absolute power needs to operate.

"The erasure of the 'other' in totalitarian regimes is foundational, not reactionary."

The "other" can only exist as an external force to be defeated, never tolerated, for tolerance risks contaminating the coherence a totalitarian regime demands. To Mr. Cook's question "Why did the Nazis feel so threatened by the Bauhaus?" then we can answer: they weren't. They were doing housekeeping.

The Bauhaus

The Bauhaus was founded in 1919 by architect Walter Gropius. Perhaps the most peculiar aspect of its artistic philosophy was just how seemingly innocuous it was. There were none of the over-the-top political manifestos of the Futurists or Dadaists. Its core principle was simple: Form must follow function.

A Peculiar Kind of Power

The Nazi Party banned Bauhaus for its perceived association with "cosmopolitan" and "Marxist" ideals. But why did the Nazis perceive such an apolitical design school in this way? I believe the answer lies not in what it represented, Ding an sich, but in what its design philosophy symbolized.

[Image: Faun by Moonlight by Léon Spilliaert]

When you look at the piece Faun by Moonlight by Léon Spilliaert, do you not feel a certain sense of dread? The devil, guiding a flock of goats? Yet, that isn't the devil. It's a faun—a benign Roman mythical creature. Two thousand years of Judeo-Christian tradition transformed a benign Roman mythical creature into the embodiment of evil itself.

"To the victors come the spoils. And in the war for cultural dominion, no more precious spoil exists than truth itself."

The Power of the Vernacular

Culture has always resided more in a people's objects than in their monuments. Cultures can borrow artistic and architectural styles without compromising their own national identity. These are symbols you look at, not objects you live with.

Yet, could the same be said for their cutlery? Their chairs, cups, beds? Can the unique cultural and environmental characteristics that shaped these everyday objects be transplanted without consequence?

Northern Europe: Chairs developed high backs and enclosed sides (winged designs) to block drafts and retain heat.

Italy: Renaissance chairs had low backs and caned seats to allow air circulation in the warmer climate.

Spain: Chairs featured leather slung between posts rather than padded seats, cooling the seater.

Hitler's obsession with symbols allowed him to recognize Bauhaus's symbolic power. A German household filled with Bauhaus furniture would look identical to a French one, to an American one. The mass-produced, minimalist chair carried no national character.

When products are designed for universal consumption, the national characteristics that once distinguished cultures erode. Identity and Status have now become indistinguishable from each other.

Bauhaus symbolized the iPhone, symbolized the Ikea, symbolized the Zara. This was a vision of a world where the intimate sphere of daily life became internationally uniform. For a regime built on racial and national distinction, this represented an impossible contradiction.

The Good War

Hitler was not unique in understanding symbols. As the dust settled, the victors of World War II began cementing the narrative they had been building. America, Britain, and Russia projected their sins onto the swastika in a purification ritual and, just like that, were exculpated of their past sins.

A collection of genocidal empires fought a genocidal empire for control of the world, won, and called it "the good war."

A Hierarchy of Suffering

The Holocaust has become perhaps the most powerful symbol of the twentieth century. But distinctive features do not create a hierarchy of suffering. A murdered Congolese child under Leopold's terror is not less dead than a murdered Jewish child in Auschwitz.

Since Oct 7th, 2023: Over 40,000 Palestinians have been killed in Gaza (conservative estimate).

Since 1967: Over 700,000 Israeli settlers have moved into the West Bank and East Jerusalem illegally.

Gaza Blockade: Over 2 million people confined to 140 square miles since 2007.

Since 1967, we have seen the same pattern of illegal occupation, murder, engineered starvation, ghettoization, and segregation, and yet to even mention this continues to be incredibly controversial.

A Moral Inequivalent

The mythologization of the Holocaust has created a framework where governments fear associating Israeli Jews with the same instincts that produced it. This symbolic power positions the Holocaust as an atrocity that cannot be compared or repeated.

And so, an unpayable moral debt is created to which all nations must answer. And they answer it with military aid used to dismember children, and with voluntary complacency to its crimes.

This is what symbols do. They control not just what we see, or what we think, but what we can feel.

The question "what am I incapable of considering in its presence?" is, for symbols, far more important than "what does it mean?" for that is how their control is exerted over you.
